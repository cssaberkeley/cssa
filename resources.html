Honors Thesis

Cognitive science majors may culminate their study in cognitive science with an honors thesis (a report on an independent research project).

The cognitive science program honors thesis page holds all official information about the honors thesis, but lacks examples of what past cognitive science theses look like.

CSSA has started an Honors Thesis Archive containing past cognitive science honors theses, in hope of making the notion a bit more concrete.

Honors Thesis Tips

  • Your thesis advisor probably has helpful suggestions if you're struggling to find a second reader.
  • Start writing your thesis as early as possible and get as many different eyes as you can on drafts.
  • Oftentimes students do their honors project in a lab they have been working in since junior year.
  • It doesn't hurt to express interest in doing such a project early on in the lab to see if it is an option.
  • Check out other tips from past thesis writers below!

If you are a graduating major and would like to include your thesis in the archive, please send an email to cssa.berkeley@gmail.com.

Attach a pdf of your thesis and the relevant information (see entries below) included in the body of the email.

What Can I Do with CogSci?

Cognitive science is one of the most interdisciplinary majors in its right, just by the sheer number of incredible pathways one can take the major through. Because you study the mind through the lenses of Psychology, Computer Science, Philosophy, and Linguistics, you aren't boxed into one career.

CogSci alumni typically go on to:

UX & Product Design
Software Engineering
Research & Academia
Medicine & Health

CogSci Career Catalyst Program

The Career Catalyst Program is for Cognitive Science majors seeking support in starting their careers after graduation.

Some of the perks of this program include networking events, assistance with seeking a position, and summer internship financial support.

Research Resources

Undergraduate Research Apprentice Program

The Undergraduate Research Apprentice Program (URAP) is Berkeley's main interface for connecting undergraduates to RA positions in labs.


Certain professors will list open projects in their labs on the program website and students can apply for the position through the website.

Research Lab Directory

While some of the labs on this list may only be available through URAP and other research programs, it still contains information about relevant labs that a CogSci major could reach out to for opportunities!

Haas Scholars Program

A similar program to SURF that provides funding for selected seniors to do research over the summer and over their senior year in preparation for a honors thesis. Applications are due late February.

Summer Undergraduate Research Fellowship

The Summer Undergraduate Research Fellowship (SURF) funds undergraduate students to do research for the summer in preparation for an honors thesis.


Applications are due in late February or early March depending on your type of project.

Tips & Tricks

Contacting Labs
+
  • There is no golden option for contacting labs. Common routes include email, visiting office hours, applying through URAP, applying through the lab website, and more.
  • Email is probably the easiest method, but it is very common not to receive a response — persistence matters.
  • Office hours allow you to create a personal connection and really demonstrate interest.
  • Be sure to prepare before visiting office hours. Read some recent publications and write down questions. You don't need to know everything — just show you're interested!
  • URAP makes the application process much more structured, but also more competitive since positions are publicly advertised.
  • Contacting graduate students directly can often be more effective than going straight to the professor. They often have more time and are more invested in RAs.
  • Some labs have instructions on how to apply on their website. Follow them — they're there for a reason!
  • There is no "right" way to get an RA position. Be persistent and engaged!
Improving Your Chances
+
  • Demonstrate genuine interest! Ask questions, have conversations with people in the lab — they want to see that you'll be engaged in the research.
  • Programming experience can really set you apart. If you have any, be sure to market it! It's used in all aspects of research and is highly desirable.
  • Prior research experience is very marketable. It's definitely possible to get a position without it, but highlight it if you have it.
  • Relevant coursework helps a lot. If you want to work in a memory lab, consider taking that professor's memory course first!
  • Statistics and Experimental Design courses are especially useful for data analysis — they can help you take on more responsibility beyond data collection.
General Tips
+
  • RA positions are generally 8–15 hrs/week. Make sure you are truly interested in the research — it's a significant time commitment!
  • Working in multiple labs throughout your undergraduate career can expose you to different research topics and styles, helping you figure out what suits you best.
  • Staying in a lab for an extended period allows you to progress further and in some cases can even lead to authorship on papers.
  • Summer research programs are incredibly valuable — they let you fully immerse yourself in the research process and give you a real sense of what full-time research is like.
